WSJ

I am currently an Android Software Engineer at the Wall Street Journal. While working there, I’ve worked on the redesign, app performance, a new purchasing and login stack, an AR ad project with Google, and refactoring to use Android Architecture Components.

Redesign

We worked closely with the Design and Editorial team to rethink how we told the news in our What’s News feed. I was the Android lead on the redesign and worked with our designer to create a performant card system for over twenty new cards that brought editorial meaning through the use of font, color, and rich images.

The UX was a challenging discovery process- we worked hard to not simply use the iOS paradigm that most are familiar with and instead advocated for Material Design standards. We flattened the UX and significantly reduced the hierarchy through a unique use of the Navigation Drawer and Bottom Navigation.

Performance

The redesign brought 20+ different cards with hundreds of variants controlled by a mobile editorial web curation tool into our feed. By abstracting out the layout composition and text styles, and by using an inversion of control Recycler View system, we were able to condense these into about 8 cards.

Every card used a completely flat XML layout with Constraint Layout, which helped us consistently keep 60fps scroll performance. We were able to do this all while supporting down to API 16 (2012 devices!) and didn’t lose a single subscriber.

Purchasing and Login

There are many ways to subscribe to WSJ- through Google Play, the Apple App Store, web, phone, etc. - which leads to a very complex login and entitlement system. I was the lead on a Single Sign On project that tied subscriptions through the Google, Amazon, and Samsung with our other entry points; the goal being able to purchase and sign on from any device on any platform with your subscription.

The technical details included setting up purchasing and receipt verification for all three Android stores as well as an Auth0-backed login and entitlement system.

Android of the Future

Working on an Android app with millions of users that originally began in 2010 brings with it a lot of history. It has seen design patterns like MVP move in and out of popularity, and changes in Google’s attitudes towards Activities and Fragments.

Refactoring to support Bottom Navigation through a single Activity; and evaluating Architecture Components, MVVM, and Kotlin without affecting our users is a big challenge. We’re aiming to change the architecture of our app to support the Android of the future.

Leap

Leap was an app for learning the English alphabet and pronunciation using Google speech recognition. First, students learn how to read and write the English alphabet with the help of audio recordings, the international phonetic alphabet, and their native alphabet. Next, students begin the Basic English unit with a curated list of vocabulary words and reading exercises. Google speech recognition evaluates their pronunciation results, which is followed by a quiz. The app was targeted for a Korean ESL audience (go Samsung!) and was first launched in Korean. Sadly it was taken off the Google Play Store after GDPR privacy regulations (we required the microphone without a privacy policy), but keep an eye out for an update!
